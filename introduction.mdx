The Multitaper Toolbox provides implementations of multitaper spectral estimation for analyzing time-series data. Originally developed for sleep neurophysiology research at the Prerau Lab, it is available in Python, MATLAB, and R with identical parameter interfaces across all three languages.

## What is the multitaper method?

The multitaper method works by averaging together multiple independent spectral estimates computed from a single segment of data. Instead of applying a single taper function to compute the spectrum, it applies multiple taper functions called **discrete prolate spheroidal sequences (DPSS)**. Because DPSS tapers are uncorrelated with each other, their resulting spectra can be averaged as if they were independent trials of the same condition — producing a final spectrum with substantially reduced variance.

This approach was developed by David Thomson in 1982 and has been shown to have superior statistical properties compared to single-taper and periodogram methods.

## Why multitaper over single-taper methods?

Standard periodogram and single-taper estimates suffer from high variance: spectral estimates at each frequency are noisy, making it difficult to resolve weak oscillations in the presence of broadband noise. The multitaper method addresses this directly:

- **Lower variance**: averaging across multiple orthogonal DPSS tapers reduces variance without sacrificing frequency resolution
- **Controlled spectral leakage**: DPSS tapers are optimally concentrated in frequency, minimizing contamination from energy at neighboring frequencies
- **Tunable resolution**: the time-half bandwidth product controls the trade-off between frequency resolution and variance reduction

In practice, this makes weak oscillations — such as sleep spindles in EEG — visible that would otherwise be buried in noise.

## What this toolbox provides

The toolbox implements the multitaper spectrogram described in the paper ["Sleep Neurophysiological Dynamics Through the Lens of Multitaper Spectral Analysis"](https://doi.org/10.1152/physiol.00062.2015) (DOI: 10.1152/physiol.00062.2015). All three implementations share the same parameter interface and produce numerically consistent results.

## Cross-language consistency

All three implementations produce numerically equivalent results:

- Python and MATLAB agree to within 10⁻¹³ on average, with standard deviation at most 10⁻¹⁰
- Python and R agree to within 10⁻¹¹ on average, with standard deviation at most 10⁻⁹

This means you can switch between languages without affecting your scientific results.
